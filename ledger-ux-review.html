The one-paragraph version

What's working, and the one thing holding it back

Ledger has a genuinely distinctive point of view. The warm, editorial aesthetic — Instrument Serif numerals on a cream field — is a deliberate rejection of the cold fintech dashboard, and it lands. The zero-based, "give every dollar a job" structure with a dedicated Giving bucket is both on-trend and personal, and the three-tab arc (plan the year → track the month → project the future) is a clean mental model. On a laptop it feels considered and calm.

Strengths to protect

Distinctive, cohesive brand Values-based "Giving" bucket Clear 3-tab arc Progress bars & over-budget states Local-first privacy Milestone ETAs on Plan Collapsible instructions

The gaps that matter most

No responsive design (1 media query) No cross-device sync Low-contrast secondary text 14-col sheet, unfrozen labels Duplicated totals on Track "Reset" as a plain text link
The headline: your CSS contains exactly one media query — and the viewport is set to device-width, so a phone renders the full desktop layout squeezed into ~390 px rather than a version built for the phone. Since Vercel serves the same build to every device, your mobile visitors currently get the hardest possible version of the app. Fixing that is the single highest-leverage move — and it's mostly CSS.

Professional scorecard

Eight dimensions, rated

Scored the way I'd assess any consumer product heading toward launch — on a 1–5 scale, weighing what a first-time user experiences, not just what the code can do.

3.1/5
Capable and charming on desktop; not yet a product you'd hand someone to open on their phone. Nearly all of the gap is concentrated in two fixable areas — responsive layout and multi-device data — rather than spread thinly across the whole app. That's good news: a focused sprint moves the overall score a lot.

▲ Current — recreation of the live Budget tab with the sheet horizontally scrolled to show the failure mode
3
Row labels scroll away.

The sheet is 14 columns wide (12 months + category + annual). It scrolls horizontally on any laptop under ~1400 px, and always on mobile — but the category column isn't frozen, so as soon as you scroll to see later months, you lose the row names. This is the classic spreadsheet sin. Freeze the first (Category) and last (Annual) columns with position:sticky.

2
"Reset" is a plain text link beside Import.

It wipes all budget and transaction data. It does show a confirm() (good), but visually it carries the same weight as Export/Import and sits one fumble away — risky on touch. Move Export/Import/Reset into an overflow "•••" menu and give Reset a warning treatment.

5
"Save budget" competes with autosave.

The app already writes every keystroke to localStorage. A prominent primary Save budget button that flips to "Saved ✓" makes users wonder whether their earlier edits were saved. Either replace it with a quiet "All changes saved" status, or make Save do something distinct — a named snapshot/version.

1
The header will overflow.

Brand + 3 nav pills + year pill + Export + Import + Reset + avatar all live in one non-wrapping row. There's no room to breathe below ~900 px; on a phone it clips. See the Mobile section for the fix.

4
Instrument Serif for KPI figures.

Gorgeous for the brand, but a thin display serif is the least scannable choice for the numbers people actually compare. Consider Geist / Geist Mono semibold for KPI values and keep the serif for titles — or keep the serif but add weight and tabular tracking.

Also strong here: the collapsible Instructions panel, the "Fill across from Jan" shortcut, per-bucket colour dots, and the allocation bar with rules-of-thumb are all thoughtful touches that reduce the intimidation of a big grid. Don't lose them.

Quick win

Freezing the Category column alone measurably improves the sheet on every screen size and is a handful of CSS lines. Pair it with a default "Annual + monthly average" compact view (expand to 12 months on demand) and the tab stops feeling like a wall of numbers.

6
The KPI strip and "July at a glance" say the same thing twice.

"Income budgeted $8,432," and the Needs/Wants/Save/Give totals, appear in both the top strip and the right panel. Pick one home for each number. A cleaner split: keep the four headline KPIs up top; make the side panel a bucket progress summary (four bars) rather than a second table of the same figures.

7
The floating pill is a mystery.

An unlabeled dark half-circle icon pinned to the right edge is one of the least discoverable patterns in UI. If it toggles the summary panel, give it a label or a tooltip, or fold it into the layout. Icon-only, edge-anchored controls get missed.

8
"click to log · 0%" repeats on every idle card.

Multiply that reminder across ~30 categories and it becomes visual static. Show the hint on hover/focus only, and let the empty state be quiet — the eye should go to the cards that have activity or are over budget.

9
"Log expense" is the #1 action — treat it like it.

On desktop it's a top-right button; fine. On a phone, the single most frequent task should be a persistent thumb-reach floating action button, not a link that scrolls away. Your Log-expense modal itself is clean and well-built — it just needs a faster door.

What's genuinely good: the per-category progress bars, the red over-budget state, the "accrued vs spent" fund cards, and the plain-language "Day 2 of 31 — 6% through the month" framing. This tab has the strongest information design in the app.

10
The chart and the controls fall out of balance.

There are six-plus stacked slider cards on the right, but the chart card on the left is short — so scrolling down leaves a tall empty gutter beside the chart (dashed). Two good fixes: (a) make the chart card sticky so it stays in view while you drag sliders, or (b) put a compact chart + headline numbers on top and lay the controls out in a 2-column grid beneath.

Keep exactly as-is: the Milestones list (Emergency fund, House down payment, Financial independence) with live ETAs and "% by yr 10" is the emotional payoff of the whole app. It turns a spreadsheet into a story. If anything, surface a one-line version of it on the Budget tab too.

The desktop + mobile question

Same Vercel build, two very different experiences

Because there are no phone breakpoints and the viewport is set to device-width, your phone doesn't get a smaller app — it gets the desktop app, shrunk. Four KPI cards fight for ~90 px each, the two-column layouts refuse to stack, and the budget grid demands sideways scrolling. On the right is the same three screens rebuilt as a phone-first layout. Nothing about the brand changes — only the arrangement.

✓
Stack the KPIs 2×2.

Four across becomes two rows of two, each figure now readable, using the one media query you'd add.

✓
Trade the 12-column grid for a month switcher.

On a phone, show one month at a time via chips (Jan…Dec + Annual). The category label stays pinned on the left; you edit a single visible column. No sideways scrolling, no lost labels.

✓
Move navigation to a bottom bar + FAB.

Budget / Track / Plan / More as a thumb-reach bar; the central "＋" is Log expense from anywhere. Export/Import/Reset live under "More."

✓
Let two-column layouts collapse to one.

Track cards, the summary panel, and Plan's chart-vs-controls each become a single stacked column below ~720 px — a few grid-template-columns:1fr rules.

Effort reality check: everything in the "after" phone is layout, not new features. One well-placed @media (max-width:720px) block plus the month-switcher for the sheet gets you ~80% of the way. The one thing CSS can't solve is data following the user between devices — that's the next section.

Strategic · beyond the UI

Data that follows the user across devices

Everything above makes the phone usable. But today all data lives in one browser's localStorage. Edit your budget on the laptop and your phone won't see it unless you manually Export a JSON file and Import it on the other device. For a product whose whole promise is "check it on my phone, plan on my desktop," that's the deepest limitation — and it's a product decision, not a CSS one.

Keep it local (privacy-first)

Lean into "your data never leaves your device." Make Export/Import effortless (one tap, QR-to-phone handoff). Honest, differentiated, zero backend.

Add optional sync

A lightweight account + hosted database (you already have a Supabase project wired up) lets the same budget open on any device, and unlocks sharing a budget with a partner. Opt-in, so the privacy story survives.

Not a recommendation to build today — just the fork in the road that the "desktop + mobile" goal eventually meets. Worth deciding on deliberately.
